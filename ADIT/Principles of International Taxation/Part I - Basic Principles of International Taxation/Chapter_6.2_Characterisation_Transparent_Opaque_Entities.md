## A. FROM RECOGNITION TO CHARACTERISATION

### 1. Building on Recognition Concepts

In Chapter 6.1, we established that states must first **recognise** a foreign entity as a legal person before they can tax it. Recognition answers the question: *Does this entity exist?*

Characterisation asks a **different** question: *Assuming this entity exists, how should it be treated for tax purposes?* Specifically:
- Should the entity itself be taxed on its income (**opaque treatment**)?
- Or should the entity be ignored and its members taxed directly (**transparent treatment**)?

This is fundamentally a **tax law** question, not a private international law question. A state may fully recognise a foreign partnership as a valid legal entity while simultaneously treating it as "transparent" for tax purposes—meaning it looks through the entity to tax the partners directly.

### 2. The Core Distinction

The distinction between transparent and opaque entities is one of the most consequential classifications in international taxation:

| Characteristic | OPAQUE Entity | TRANSPARENT Entity |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Tax subject** | Entity itself is taxpayer | Members/owners are taxpayers |
| **Income taxation** | Taxed at entity level | Flows through to members |
| **Loss utilisation** | Trapped in entity | Available to members |
| **Distribution taxation** | Dividends taxed when distributed | No distribution event—already taxed |
| **Treaty access** | Entity may be treaty resident | Entity NOT resident; members may be |
| **Examples** | Companies, corporations | Partnerships, LLCs (often) |
| **Alternative terms** | Non-transparent, fiscally opaque | Flow-through, fiscally transparent, pass-through |

Understanding this distinction is essential because the same entity may be characterised differently by different states—creating the foundation for hybrid mismatches.

## B. STATE PRACTICE IN ENTITY CHARACTERISATION

### 1. The US "Check-the-Box" Regime

The United States operates perhaps the most flexible entity classification system globally through its **check-the-box regulations** (Treasury Regulations §301.7701-2 and -3), effective since 1997.

**How it works:**

```
US CHECK-THE-BOX FRAMEWORK
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STEP 1: Is entity a "per se" corporation?
        → Listed entities (Inc., AG, SA, PLC, etc.) = Corporation
        → CANNOT elect different treatment

STEP 2: If NOT per se corporation = "Eligible entity"
        → CAN elect classification by filing Form 8832

DEFAULT RULES (if no election made):
┌─────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Entity Type         │ Default Classification                  │
├─────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Domestic, 2+ owners │ Partnership (transparent)              │
│ Domestic, 1 owner   │ Disregarded entity (transparent)       │
│ Foreign, all limited│ Association (corporation/opaque)       │
│ Foreign, some       │ Partnership (transparent)              │
│ unlimited liability │                                        │
└─────────────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────┘

ELECTION OPTIONS:
• Partnership → Corporation (or vice versa)
• Disregarded entity → Corporation (or vice versa)
• 60-month limitation on re-election
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

**Tax planning significance**: The check-the-box rules enable US taxpayers to elect different treatment for the same legal entity. A UK private limited company could elect to be treated as a partnership or disregarded entity for US tax purposes, while remaining opaque for UK purposes—creating a hybrid mismatch.

### 2. UK Approach: Following Legal Form

The UK traditionally follows a **legal form** approach to entity characterisation. Whether an entity is transparent or opaque depends primarily on its legal structure under UK law or, for foreign entities, its characteristics when mapped against UK concepts.

**UK characterisation framework:**

| Entity Type | UK Treatment | Rationale |
|-------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Limited company** (UK) | Opaque | Separate legal personality |
| **LLP** (UK) | Transparent | Statutory transparency for tax |
| **General partnership** | Transparent | No separate legal personality (in England) |
| **Scottish partnership** | Complex | Has legal personality but transparent for tax |
| **Foreign entity** | Depends on characteristics | Mapped to closest UK equivalent |

**The UK LLP anomaly**: UK Limited Liability Partnerships demonstrate that legal personality and tax transparency can coexist. An LLP is a separate legal person (can own property, sue, be sued) but is fiscally transparent—partners are taxed on their share of profits as if the LLP did not exist.

### 3. German Practice: Structural Approach

Germany traditionally characterises entities based on their **structural similarity** to German entity types, applying detailed comparison criteria:

**German classification criteria:**
- Centralised management structure
- Limited liability of members
- Free transferability of interests
- Continuity of existence
- Whether profits can be distributed

An entity exhibiting corporate characteristics (limited liability, centralised management, transferable shares) is typically treated as opaque. Partnership-like entities are transparent.

### 4. Overview of Other Major Jurisdictions

| Jurisdiction | Approach | Notable Features |
|--------------|----------|------------------|
| **France** | Legal form | Société civile generally transparent; SA/SAS opaque |
| **Netherlands** | Economic substance | CV (commanditaire vennootschap) can be transparent or opaque |
| **Australia** | Follows legal form | Trusts often transparent; companies opaque |
| **Japan** | Entity type | GK (godo kaisha) generally opaque; partnerships transparent |
| **Singapore** | UK-influenced | Limited partnerships transparent; companies opaque |

## C. HYBRID ENTITIES AND MISMATCHES

### 1. What Creates a Hybrid Entity?

A **hybrid entity** arises when the same legal arrangement is characterised differently by two or more jurisdictions. This is not a characteristic of the entity itself but of how it is perceived across borders.

```
HYBRID ENTITY FORMATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

EXAMPLE: Delaware LLC with UK parent company

                         DELAWARE LLC
                              │
        ┌─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┐
        │                                           │
   US PERSPECTIVE                             UK PERSPECTIVE
   (State of formation)                       (Owner jurisdiction)
        │                                           │
   Default: Transparent                        Opaque
   (unless elected                            (corporate characteristics)
   otherwise)                                       │
        │                                           │
   Income flows to UK parent               LLC treated as company
   No US tax at LLC level                  No UK tax until dividend

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

**Result**: Income earned by the LLC may not be taxed anywhere:
- US sees it as flowing to the UK parent → no US tax
- UK sees the LLC as opaque → no UK tax until distribution
- = **Double non-taxation** (D/NI outcome)

### 2. Types of Hybrid Mismatches

The OECD's BEPS Action 2 identifies several categories of hybrid mismatches:

| Mismatch Type | Description | Tax Outcome |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Deduction/No Inclusion (D/NI)** | Payment deductible in payer jurisdiction but not included in payee jurisdiction | Single deduction, no taxation |
| **Double Deduction (DD)** | Same expense deducted in two jurisdictions | Two deductions for one cost |
| **Indirect D/NI** | Mismatch through intermediary entities | Deduction chains without inclusion |

**Hybrid entities** most commonly produce **D/NI mismatches** where:
1. Payer jurisdiction allows deduction (e.g., interest payment)
2. Payee jurisdiction does not tax the receipt (e.g., because entity is transparent and income attributed to exempt owner)

### 3. Reverse Hybrids

A **reverse hybrid** is the mirror image: an entity treated as opaque in its jurisdiction of establishment but transparent in the investor's jurisdiction.

```
REVERSE HYBRID STRUCTURE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

EXAMPLE: German KG with US corporate investor

                         GERMAN KG
                    (Established in Germany)
                              │
        ┌─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┐
        │                                           │
   GERMAN PERSPECTIVE                         US PERSPECTIVE
   (Entity jurisdiction)                      (Investor jurisdiction)
        │                                           │
   Transparent                                Opaque
   (Kommanditgesellschaft                     (German KG mapped as
   is partnership)                            foreign corporation)
        │                                           │
   No tax at KG level                         US investor not taxed
   Income attributed to partners              until KG distributes

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

**Result**: Income may escape taxation entirely:
- Germany attributes income to US investor (transparent) → no German tax on KG
- US treats KG as opaque → no US tax until distribution
- = **Reverse hybrid mismatch**

## D. TREATY APPLICATION AND TRANSPARENT ENTITIES

### 1. Article 1(2) OECD Model Tax Convention

The 2017 OECD Model Tax Convention added Article 1(2) specifically to address fiscally transparent entities:

> *"Income derived by or through an entity or arrangement that is treated as wholly or partly fiscally transparent under the tax law of either Contracting State shall be considered to be income of a resident of a Contracting State but only to the extent that the income is treated, for purposes of taxation by that State, as the income of a resident of that State."*

**What Article 1(2) achieves:**
- Clarifies that transparent entity treatment does not preclude treaty benefits
- Income "flows through" to the resident who is taxed on it
- That resident (e.g., partner) can claim treaty benefits
- Addresses the "liable to tax" requirement in Article 4(1)

### 2. The "Liable to Tax" Connection

As discussed in Chapter 6.1, Article 4(1) requires a person to be "liable to tax" to qualify as a resident. Transparent entities raise a fundamental question: *Is a transparent entity "liable to tax" when it pays no tax itself?*

The June 2022 exam directly tested this issue:

```
EXAM FOCUS: June 2022 Q1 - "Liable to Tax" and Transparent Entities
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

QUESTION PATTERN: Whether "liable to tax" requires actual payment

KEY POINTS FROM SUGGESTED SOLUTION:

1. Article 1(2) provides specific rule for transparent entities:
   • Income through transparent entity = income of resident
   • To extent taxed AS income of resident

2. Partnership classification mismatch:
   • One state treats partnership as opaque
   • Other state treats it as transparent
   • Article 1(2) allows the state treating it as transparent
     to attribute treaty benefits to the partners

3. "Liable to tax" ≠ "subject to tax":
   • Person need not ACTUALLY PAY tax
   • Must be within scope of taxing jurisdiction
   • Exemptions, losses, allowances don't break "liability"

4. Partner in transparent partnership:
   • Partner is "liable to tax" on partnership income
   • Partner can claim treaty benefits
   • Partnership itself need not be treaty resident
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

### 3. Classification Conflicts and Treaty Application

When states disagree on entity characterisation, complex treaty issues arise:

| Scenario | Source State View | Residence State View | Treaty Outcome |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| A | Entity opaque | Entity opaque | Standard Article 10/11/12 apply to entity |
| B | Entity opaque | Entity transparent | Conflict: withholding on "dividend" but no recipient |
| C | Entity transparent | Entity opaque | Conflict: source state looks to partners but residence state does not |
| D | Entity transparent | Entity transparent | Both look through to partners—article depends on income type |

**Commentary guidance**: The OECD Commentary to Article 1 provides that the residence state's characterisation should generally prevail for treaty benefit purposes, but this remains an area of significant interpretive difficulty.

## E. BEPS ACTION 2: NEUTRALISING HYBRID MISMATCHES

### 1. Overview of BEPS Action 2

BEPS Action 2 (2015 Final Report, updated 2017) provides recommendations to neutralise hybrid mismatch arrangements. The action targets arrangements that exploit characterisation differences to achieve:
- Deduction with no inclusion (D/NI)
- Double deduction (DD)

**Two-part structure:**
- **Part I**: Recommendations for domestic law changes
- **Part II**: Recommendations for treaty changes

### 2. The Linking Rules Approach

BEPS Action 2 implements **linking rules** that link the tax treatment in one jurisdiction to the treatment in another:

```
LINKING RULES HIERARCHY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

FOR D/NI (Deduction/No Inclusion):

PRIMARY RULE (applies first):
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ PAYER JURISDICTION: Deny deduction if payment not included     │
│ in payee jurisdiction due to hybrid mismatch                   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              │
                              ▼ (if primary rule not applied)

DEFENSIVE RULE (applies second):
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ PAYEE JURISDICTION: Require inclusion if deduction allowed     │
│ in payer jurisdiction due to hybrid mismatch                   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

FOR DD (Double Deduction):

PRIMARY RULE:
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ PARENT JURISDICTION: Deny deduction to extent also deducted    │
│ in subsidiary jurisdiction                                      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              │
                              ▼ (if primary rule not applied)

DEFENSIVE RULE:
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ SUBSIDIARY JURISDICTION: Deny deduction to extent also         │
│ deducted in parent jurisdiction                                │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

## F. CFC RULES AND ENTITY CHARACTERISATION

### 1. Two CFC Approaches: Transparent vs Opaque Treatment

Controlled Foreign Company (CFC) rules can treat the CFC as either transparent or opaque, with different consequences:

| Approach | Description | Consequences |
|----------|-------------|--------------|
| **Entity approach** (opaque) | CFC remains separate entity; income attributed to shareholder | CFC retains legal personality; losses not passed through |
| **Transactional approach** (transparent) | "Lift the veil"; CFC treated as branch of parent | CFC loses separate status; losses may be passed through |

As noted in the December 2022 exam solutions:
> *"DAA recognises the CFC as opaque for tax purposes but its income is attributed to the shareholders. This approach differs from [lifting the veil] as the DAA retains the CFC's separate legal personality and residence status but does not normally attribute losses to the shareholder."*

### 2. Impact on Treaty Application

CFC rules interact with entity characterisation for treaty purposes:
- If CFC treated as opaque: CFC may claim treaty benefits in its residence state
- If CFC treated as transparent: No CFC treaty residence; income attributed to parent
- Hybrid treatment: Complex interactions with both CFC jurisdiction and parent jurisdiction treaties

## G. PILLAR TWO AND ENTITY CHARACTERISATION

### 1. Flow-Through Entity Treatment Under GloBE Rules

The OECD's Pillar Two GloBE rules contain specific provisions for flow-through entities:

**Key definitions:**
- **Flow-through entity**: Entity treated as transparent in its jurisdiction of establishment
- **Tax transparent entity**: Flow-through entity also treated as transparent by the owner's jurisdiction
- **Reverse hybrid entity**: Flow-through entity treated as opaque by the owner's jurisdiction

**Treatment under GloBE:**
- Tax transparent entities: Income and taxes attributed to constituent entity owners
- Reverse hybrids: May be subject to UTPR (Undertaxed Profits Rule) adjustments
- Same entity can be classified differently depending on owner's jurisdiction

### 2. Complexity in Multinational Groups

Under Pillar Two, a single flow-through entity may have different characterisations for different owners located in different jurisdictions—creating computational complexity for determining the effective tax rate and any top-up tax liability.

## H. PRACTICAL APPLICATION FRAMEWORK

### 1. Analytical Steps for Exam Questions

When analysing entity characterisation issues:

```
STEP-BY-STEP ANALYSIS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STEP 1: IDENTIFY THE ENTITY
   • What type of entity? (LLC, LP, GmbH, etc.)
   • Where established?
   • Who are the owners and where are they located?

STEP 2: DETERMINE CHARACTERISATION IN EACH JURISDICTION
   • Entity jurisdiction: transparent or opaque?
   • Owner jurisdiction(s): transparent or opaque?
   • Source jurisdiction (if different): transparent or opaque?

STEP 3: IDENTIFY ANY MISMATCH
   • Same characterisation everywhere? → No hybrid issue
   • Different characterisations? → Hybrid entity exists

STEP 4: ANALYSE TAX CONSEQUENCES
   • Who is taxed on income? (entity or members)
   • When is tax triggered? (accrual or distribution)
   • Is there double taxation or double non-taxation?

STEP 5: CONSIDER ANTI-AVOIDANCE RULES
   • Do BEPS Action 2 rules apply?
   • Are linking rules triggered?
   • CFC implications?

STEP 6: DETERMINE TREATY APPLICATION
   • Who can claim treaty benefits?
   • Article 1(2) application?
   • Which treaty applies to which income?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

## Common Exam Errors to Avoid

| Error | Why It Loses Marks | Correct Approach |
|-------|-------------------|------------------|
| Assuming entity classification is universal | Different states classify same entity differently | Analyse characterisation in EACH relevant jurisdiction |
| Confusing recognition with characterisation | Recognition = existence; characterisation = tax treatment | Keep concepts separate (Chapter 6.1 vs 6.2) |
| Ignoring Article 1(2) for partnership questions | Key provision for treaty access | Always consider for transparent entity treaty questions |
| Applying only primary OR defensive rules | Rules operate in hierarchy | Explain both; defensive only if primary not applied |
| Treating "liable to tax" as requiring payment | Well-established it does not | Explain difference between liable and subject to tax |
| Missing hybrid entity in fact pattern | Characterisation differences create exam issues | Check each jurisdiction's view of entity |
