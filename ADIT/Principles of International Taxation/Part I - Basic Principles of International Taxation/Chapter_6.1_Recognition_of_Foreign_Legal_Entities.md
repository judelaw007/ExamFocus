## 1. The Fundamental Question: What Is a "Legal Entity"?

### 1. Why Recognition Matters for International Taxation

Before a state can tax an entity—or before a treaty can apply to it—the entity must first be **recognised** as a legal person capable of bearing tax obligations. This recognition question sits at the intersection of private international law and tax law, creating a preliminary hurdle that shapes all subsequent tax analysis.

The recognition question asks: *Does this arrangement constitute a separate legal entity that can be the subject of taxation?* The answer determines:

| If Recognised as Separate Entity | If NOT Recognised as Separate Entity |
|----------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Entity itself is the taxpayer | Underlying members/owners are taxpayers |
| Entity may be treaty-resident | Entity cannot claim treaty benefits |
| Separate liability for tax debts | Members directly liable |
| Corporate tax applies to profits | Income flows through to members |
| Dividends when distributed | No distribution event—direct attribution |

This foundational question explains why the December 2022 exam explicitly noted that Algero was "recognised as a separate legal entity for tax and company law purposes"—establishing the necessary premise for treaty residence analysis.

### 2. The Source of Recognition Rules

Recognition of legal entities is governed by **private international law** (conflict of laws), not by tax law directly. Each state's private international law rules determine which law governs the existence and legal capacity of an entity—this is known as the **lex societatis** (the law of the company).

The critical insight is that tax law inherits its understanding of entities from private international law. When a tax statute refers to a "company" or "corporation," it relies on private international law to determine whether a particular arrangement qualifies as such.

## 2. Two Competing Theories of Recognition

### 1. The Incorporation Theory

Under the **incorporation theory**, a company's legal existence and internal affairs are governed by the law of the state where it was incorporated—regardless of where it actually operates or where its management is located.

**Key characteristics:**
- Place of registration/incorporation determines applicable law
- Founders choose governing law by choosing incorporation jurisdiction
- Physical location of operations is irrelevant to legal existence
- Emphasises **party autonomy** and legal certainty

**Jurisdictions applying incorporation theory:**
- United Kingdom
- United States
- Ireland
- Netherlands
- Most common law countries

**Practical effect**: A company incorporated in Delaware remains a Delaware corporation for recognition purposes even if all its directors, shareholders, and operations are in Germany. The German tax authority must recognise it as a valid legal entity if German private international law follows the incorporation theory.

### 2. The Real Seat Theory (Sitztheorie)

Under the **real seat theory**, a company's legal existence is governed by the law of the state where its actual headquarters or effective management is located—the "real seat" or *siège réel*.

**Key characteristics:**
- Place of effective management determines applicable law
- Incorporation jurisdiction is secondary to actual operations
- Emphasises **third-party protection** and regulatory control
- State where business genuinely occurs has authority

**Jurisdictions historically applying real seat theory:**
- Germany
- Austria
- Belgium
- Portugal
- Many civil law countries

**Critical consequence**: Under the real seat theory, a company incorporated abroad but managed domestically may **not be recognised** as a valid foreign company. Instead, it may be treated as:
- A domestic partnership (with unlimited liability for members)
- A domestic company (subject to domestic formation requirements)
- A non-entity (void for failure to meet local requirements)

### 3. The German Example: Dramatic Consequences

The German Federal Supreme Court's approach illustrates the real seat theory's impact. In a landmark 2008 decision, a Swiss stock corporation (Aktiengesellschaft) with its real seat in Germany was held to be treated as a **German partnership** for legal purposes. Since it was not incorporated under German law, it could not be treated as a German corporation, with the consequence that shareholders faced **unlimited personal liability** for company debts.

For tax purposes, this meant the entity was transparent—income flowed through to members rather than being taxed at entity level.

## 3. EU Law Developments: Convergence Toward Incorporation

### 1. ECJ Case Law and Freedom of Establishment

Within the European Union, the real seat theory has been significantly curtailed by European Court of Justice (ECJ) jurisprudence on freedom of establishment (Article 49 TFEU). Key cases established that EU Member States cannot refuse to recognise companies validly incorporated in other Member States:

| Case | Key Principle |
|------|---------------|
| **Centros (1999)** | A company validly formed in one Member State must be recognised by other Member States |
| **Überseering (2002)** | Refusing to recognise a company's legal capacity violates freedom of establishment |
| **Inspire Art (2003)** | Member States cannot impose domestic formation requirements on foreign EU companies |

**Result**: Most EU Member States now effectively apply the incorporation theory to companies incorporated within the EU/EEA, even if they formally retain real seat theory in their statutes.

### 2. Residual Application of Real Seat Theory

The real seat theory remains relevant in the EU context for:
- Companies incorporated in **non-EU/EEA states** (third countries)
- Questions of **tax residence** (as opposed to legal recognition)
- Certain aspects of **creditor protection** and insolvency law

This creates a bifurcated system in some jurisdictions: incorporation theory for EU companies, real seat theory for third-country companies.

## 4. Recognition and Tax Residence: The Connection

### 1. Recognition Precedes Residence

Recognition and residence are related but distinct concepts:

| Recognition | Residence |
|-------------|-----------|
| Does the entity **exist** as a legal person? | **Where** is the entity resident for tax purposes? |
| Determined by private international law | Determined by tax law (domestic or treaty) |
| Binary question: yes/no | Multiple states may claim residence |
| Prerequisite for taxation | Determines scope of tax liability |

A state must first recognise an entity as existing before it can determine whether that entity is resident. The December 2022 exam question on Algero demonstrates this sequence: only after establishing Algero was "recognised as a separate legal entity" could the analysis proceed to residence under domestic law and treaty tie-breaker rules.

### 2. Cross-Reference to Residence Concepts

As established in Chapter 3.2, states use various criteria to determine corporate residence:
- **Place of incorporation** (UK, US)
- **Place of effective management/POEM** (Germany, South Africa)
- **Central management and control** (UK common law test)

Note the overlap: the "real seat" concept in recognition (private international law) resembles the "place of effective management" concept in tax residence (tax law). However:
- A state following incorporation theory for recognition may still use POEM for tax residence
- Recognition determines IF an entity exists; residence determines WHERE it is taxed

## 5. Recognition Mismatches and Tax Consequences

### 1. When States Disagree on Recognition

Different states may reach different conclusions about whether an entity exists. This creates **recognition mismatches** with significant tax consequences:

**Scenario**: An LLC formed in Delaware, with management in Germany

| Perspective | Under US View | Under Traditional German View |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| **Entity status** | Valid LLC exists | May not be recognised as valid entity |
| **Lex societatis** | Delaware law | German law (real seat) |
| **Tax treatment** | Depends on US classification | Likely transparent (partnership) |
| **Treaty access** | US resident for treaty | Cannot be treaty resident |

### 2. Consequences of Non-Recognition

When a state does not recognise a foreign entity:

1. **No entity-level taxation**: The entity is "looked through" and members are taxed directly
2. **No treaty access**: The entity cannot be a "person" under Article 1 or "resident" under Article 4
3. **Withholding tax issues**: Payments to the entity may be treated as payments to members
4. **Anti-avoidance provisions**: May trigger CFC or similar rules earlier than expected

### 3. Link to Hybrid Mismatches

Recognition mismatches are a primary driver of **hybrid entity** outcomes (covered in Chapter 6.2). When one state treats an arrangement as a company and another treats it as transparent, planning opportunities—and double taxation risks—emerge. BEPS Action 2 and its implementing measures (linking rules) respond specifically to these situations.

## 6. Practical Application: Exam Scenarios

### 1. How Recognition Appears in Exam Questions

Recognition rarely appears as a standalone question but forms the foundation for more complex analysis. Watch for phrases like:

- "X Ltd is a company incorporated and tax resident in Country A"
- "X is recognised as a separate legal entity under the laws of Country A"
- "X is a tax resident under the domestic law of Country B due to its incorporation there"

These statements establish the recognition premise, allowing analysis to proceed to residence, treaty application, and tax consequences.

### 2. December 2022 Q6 Analysis

The question stated Algero was:
- "recognised as a separate legal entity for tax and company law purposes under the laws of Country A"
- "also a tax resident under the domestic law of Country B, due to its incorporation there"

**What these statements mean:**
1. Country A recognises Algero (probably using real seat/POEM test given management is there)
2. Country B recognises Algero (using incorporation theory)
3. Both states claim tax residence under domestic law → dual residence
4. Treaty Article 4 tie-breaker must resolve

**The recognition issue**: If Country A applied strict real seat theory and Country B didn't recognise the entity at all, the analysis would fundamentally differ. The question's explicit statement avoided this complication.

## 7. Common Exam Errors to Avoid

| Error | Why It Loses Marks | Correct Approach |
|-------|-------------------|------------------|
| Confusing recognition with residence | Different concepts with different sources | Recognition = existence (private law); Residence = location (tax law) |
| Ignoring recognition when analysing treaties | Entity must be recognised to be treaty-resident | Note recognition before proceeding to Article 4 analysis |
| Assuming all states recognise foreign entities | Some states may not recognise under real seat theory | Consider whether recognition might be disputed |
| Treating recognition as a tax law question | It's private international law | Acknowledge the lex societatis concept |
